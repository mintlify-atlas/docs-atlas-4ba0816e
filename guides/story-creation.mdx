## Planning your story

Before you start recording, think about the story you want to tell. Even simple stories benefit from a bit of planning.

### Start with a simple premise

Great toy movies often have straightforward setups:

- An adventure to find something (treasure, a lost friend)
- A problem to solve (rescue mission, mystery)
- A competition or challenge (race, talent show)
- A day in the life of your toys

<Tip>
  Keep your first movie simple — 2-3 scenes with a clear beginning, middle, and end.
</Tip>

### Break your story into scenes

Each scene should have:

- **A location** — where is this happening? (forest, castle, playground)
- **Characters** — which toys appear in this scene?
- **Action** — what happens? (the hero finds a clue, friends argue, someone makes a discovery)
- **Dialogue** — what do the characters say?

<Steps>
  <Step title="Write down your scene ideas">
    Make a simple list of scenes. For example:
    1. Superhero discovers a problem in the city
    2. Superhero travels to the villain's castle
    3. Final confrontation and victory
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Choose backgrounds for each scene">
    Think about what setting matches each part of your story. You'll describe these to the voice agent later.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Practice your dialogue">
    Run through what each character will say. You can improvise, but knowing the general direction helps.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Using the voice agent effectively

Cinemachine uses a voice agent powered by Gemini Live API to have a conversation with you about your story. This helps the system understand what backgrounds to generate and how to bring your toys to life.

### How the voice conversation works

The voice agent will ask you questions about:

- What's happening in the story
- Where the scene takes place
- What kind of background you want
- Details about your toys (what kind of eyes they should have, personality traits)

<Note>
  The voice agent listens and responds in real-time, making it feel like a natural conversation.
</Note>

### Describing backgrounds

When the agent asks about your scene setting, be specific but concise:

**Good descriptions:**
- "A dark, spooky forest with tall trees and fog"
- "A bright castle throne room with golden walls"
- "A futuristic space station with glowing panels"
- "An underwater scene with coral and bubbles"

**Less helpful descriptions:**
- "Somewhere outside" (too vague)
- "Like in that movie with the thing" (agent can't see references)

<Tip>
  Include mood and atmosphere in your description. Words like "spooky," "cheerful," "mysterious," or "action-packed" help create the right feel.
</Tip>

### Describing your toy characters

The voice agent helps determine:

- **Eye style** — should they have big cartoon eyes, realistic eyes, or something else?
- **Facial features** — what personality should the eyes convey? (friendly, fierce, curious)
- **Animation style** — should the toy bounce energetically or move calmly?

You can specify these details, or let the agent suggest options based on your toy type.

## Choosing and generating backgrounds

Cinemachine generates fantasy backgrounds using Nanobanana image generation. Your original room background is completely replaced.

### One background per scene

Backgrounds are generated once per scene and remain static:

- This keeps processing fast and results consistent
- If you want multiple backgrounds, record multiple scenes
- The static background works because your camera is fixed — it looks natural

### Background generation tips

**Style consistency**

If you're making a multi-scene movie, keep background styles consistent:

- Same art style (realistic, cartoon, painterly)
- Consistent color palette across scenes
- Similar level of detail

**Matching toy aesthetic**

Consider how the background complements your toys:

- Cartoon-style toys might look best with illustrated backgrounds
- Realistic action figures might suit photorealistic or cinematic backgrounds
- Mix and match to find what looks coolest to you

<Warning>
  Very busy or detailed backgrounds can sometimes distract from the toy action. Simpler backgrounds often work better.
</Warning>

## Managing multiple toys in a scene

When you have multiple characters, coordination makes the story clearer.

### Introducing characters

Bring toys into the scene one at a time:

1. Start with one toy and let Cinemachine track it
2. Bring in the second toy
3. Continue with additional toys if needed

This helps the segmentation system track each toy separately.

### Dialogue with multiple characters

When characters talk to each other:

- Move the speaking toy slightly (gestures, head movements)
- Keep other toys relatively still
- Change your voice for each character to distinguish them
- Give each character time to "speak" before the next one responds

<Tip>
  Think of it like puppetry — one puppet moves and talks while others react and wait their turn.
</Tip>

### Action scenes with multiple toys

For battles, chases, or group activities:

- Move one toy at a time for clearer action
- Use quick cuts between scenes if you need complex choreography
- Remember that each toy is tracked separately, so overlapping movement is okay but trickier

## Adding eyes and bringing toys to life

Cinemachine adds eyes and mouths to your toys to make them expressive and alive.

### Eye placement

You can either:

- Let the voice agent help detect where eyes should go
- Specify the face area yourself during the conversation

The eyes will track with the toy as it moves through each frame.

### Lip sync and dialogue

The system automatically syncs the toy's mouth to your recorded audio:

- Mouth movements match your dialogue timing
- The mouth opens and closes naturally with speech patterns
- Non-speaking toys keep their mouths neutral

<Note>
  Lip sync is generated using audio analysis, so clear audio recording (see [Recording tips](/guides/recording-tips)) gives better results.
</Note>

### Body animation

Subtle animations make toys feel more alive:

- **Squash and stretch** — slight deformation as toys move, like in classic animation
- **Breathing motion** — gentle movement when toys are still
- **Speaking bounce** — toys bounce slightly when they talk, driven by audio energy

These animations are automatic and subtle — they enhance without being distracting.

## Story structure tips

### The three-act structure

Even short toy movies work well with this classic structure:

**Act 1: Setup** (one scene)
- Introduce characters and the situation
- Establish the problem or goal

**Act 2: Confrontation** (one or two scenes)
- Characters face challenges
- Action and conflict

**Act 3: Resolution** (one scene)
- Problem solved or goal achieved
- Satisfying ending

### Pacing your story

Keep the energy moving:

- Start scenes with action or interesting dialogue
- Keep scenes focused on one main event
- End scenes with a reason to continue (cliffhanger, question, discovery)

<Tip>
  If a scene feels slow, it might be too long. Try cutting it into two shorter scenes or removing unnecessary dialogue.
</Tip>

## Creative ideas to try

### Genre mashups

Mix different toy types for unexpected stories:

- Dinosaurs in space
- Superheroes in a medieval castle
- Cars having a cooking competition

### Series and sequels

Once you've made one movie, continue the story:

- Create episodic adventures with recurring characters
- Build on previous events
- Develop character relationships over time

### Collaborative storytelling

Work with friends or siblings:

- Each person controls different toys
- Take turns planning scenes
- Combine different recording sessions into one movie
