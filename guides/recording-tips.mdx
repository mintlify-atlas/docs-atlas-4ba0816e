## Setting up your recording space

To create the best movies with Cinemachine, you'll need a fixed camera setup where only the toys move. This approach gives you the cleanest results and makes it easier to bring your toys to life.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Choose a stable surface">
    Find a table, desk, or floor area where you can position your camera on a tripod or stable mount. The camera should not move at all during recording.
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Position your camera">
    Set up your camera pointing at the play area. Make sure the entire area where toys will move is visible in the frame.
    
    <Tip>
      Use a smartphone on a tripod or propped against books. You don't need expensive equipment!
    </Tip>
  </Step>
  
  <Step title="Capture a reference frame">
    Before you start playing, record a few seconds of the empty scene with no toys or hands visible. This reference frame helps Cinemachine understand what to remove later.
    
    <Note>
      The reference frame is captured once at the beginning and used throughout the scene.
    </Note>
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Lighting your scene

Good lighting makes a huge difference in how well Cinemachine can segment your toys and create clean cutouts.

### Natural light works best

- Record near a window during daytime for even, natural lighting
- Avoid direct sunlight that creates harsh shadows
- Overcast days actually provide the most even lighting

### Indoor lighting tips

- Use multiple light sources to reduce shadows
- Position lights to the sides rather than directly overhead
- Avoid backlighting (light behind the toy) which creates silhouettes

<Warning>
  Avoid flickering lights or changing lighting conditions during a scene. Keep lighting consistent from your reference frame through the entire recording.
</Warning>

## Getting clean toy segmentation

Cinemachine uses AI to separate the toy from everything else in the frame. Here's how to get the best results:

### Choose the right background

- Use a plain, solid-colored background (tablecloth, poster board, or fabric)
- Avoid busy patterns, textures, or colors that match your toy
- Contrasting colors work best — if your toy is dark, use a light background

### Handle the toys carefully

Since Cinemachine segments only the toy and discards everything else (including hands), you want to minimize hand-toy overlap:

- Hold toys from the bottom or sides when possible
- Use quick, deliberate movements rather than keeping hands on the toy constantly
- Practice moving the toy smoothly to avoid motion blur

<Tip>
  The AI is designed to track the toy and exclude hands from the mask, but cleaner separation makes better results.
</Tip>

### Dealing with multiple toys

When you have multiple toys in one scene:

- Introduce toys one at a time so Cinemachine can track each one
- Keep toys separated when possible — overlapping toys can be tricky
- Move one toy at a time during dialogue for clearer action

## Audio recording tips

Your voice brings the characters to life, and Cinemachine will add lip sync to match your dialogue.

### Recording clear dialogue

- Speak clearly and at a moderate pace
- Record in a quiet room to minimize background noise
- Position yourself close enough to the camera's microphone
- Avoid rustling, tapping, or other noise while recording

### Character voices

Feel free to do different voices for different toys! The audio you record will be used as-is, so:

- Practice character voices before recording
- Keep voices consistent for each toy throughout the story
- Have fun and be expressive — energy shows up in the final movie

<Note>
  Currently, Cinemachine keeps your original voice. In future versions, you may be able to generate character voices.
</Note>

## Frame rate and resolution

### Camera settings

- Use 1080p resolution or higher if your camera supports it
- 30 fps (frames per second) is perfectly fine for most movies
- Keep the camera in landscape orientation unless you're specifically making vertical content

### Recording length

Each scene should be manageable for processing:

- Aim for 30 seconds to 2 minutes per scene
- Record multiple shorter scenes rather than one long take
- You can combine scenes later to make a longer movie

<Tip>
  Shorter scenes process faster and give you more flexibility when editing your story.
</Tip>

## Troubleshooting common issues

### Toy isn't being tracked properly

- Ensure the toy contrasts well with the background
- Check that lighting is even across the play area
- Avoid toys that are transparent, reflective, or very small

### Hands showing up in the final cut

- Minimize how long hands stay on the toy
- Hold toys from areas that won't be visible (bottom, back)
- Use faster, more deliberate movements

### Background looks inconsistent

- Make sure your reference frame matches the scene lighting
- Capture a new reference frame if lighting has changed
- Keep the camera completely still — even small movements affect results
