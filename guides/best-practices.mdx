## Choosing the right toys

Not all toys work equally well with Cinemachine. Here's what to look for when selecting characters for your movie.

### Toys that work best

**Solid, opaque toys with clear shapes**

- Action figures
- Plastic dinosaurs and animals
- Cars and vehicles
- Building block characters (LEGO minifigs, etc.)
- Stuffed animals with defined edges

**Good color contrast**

- Toys with distinct colors that stand out from backgrounds
- Multi-colored toys are easier to segment than monochrome ones
- Bright, saturated colors generally work better than pastels

**Moderate size**

- Toys should be large enough to see facial features clearly
- But not so large they dominate the entire frame
- A good rule: toys between 2 inches and 12 inches tall work well

<Tip>
  Test different toys in your setup. Some unexpected toys create amazing results!
</Tip>

### Toys that are challenging

**Transparent or translucent toys**

- Clear plastic, glass, or see-through materials are hard to segment
- Light passes through them unpredictably
- If you must use them, try stronger lighting from the sides

**Very reflective toys**

- Shiny metallic surfaces reflect the environment
- Chrome or mirror finishes can confuse segmentation
- Matte or semi-gloss toys work much better

**Tiny or highly detailed toys**

- Very small toys (under 1 inch) may be hard to track
- Extremely intricate details might not render well after segmentation
- Facial features could be too small for effective eye/mouth placement

**Flat or paper-based toys**

- Paper dolls, flat cards, or 2D cutouts are tricky
- They disappear when turned sideways
- 3D toys with volume work much better

<Warning>
  Avoid toys with hair-like materials (doll hair, fur, feathers) at the edges. These create fuzzy segmentation boundaries.
</Warning>

## Audio quality best practices

Clear audio is essential for good lip sync and an engaging final movie.

### Recording environment

**Choose a quiet space**

- Turn off fans, air conditioning, or appliances if possible
- Close windows to reduce outside noise
- Avoid rooms with echo (bathrooms, empty rooms with hard floors)
- Carpeted rooms or rooms with furniture absorb sound better

**Timing matters**

- Record when your environment is naturally quiet
- Avoid times when neighbors, traffic, or household activity is loud
- Let family members know you're recording

### Microphone technique

**Distance and position**

- Stay within 2-3 feet of the camera/microphone
- Speak toward the microphone, not away from it
- Maintain consistent distance throughout the scene

**Voice projection**

- Speak clearly and at moderate volume
- Don't whisper (creates poor audio) or shout (creates distortion)
- Enunciate words, especially for character voices

<Tip>
  Do a test recording first. Watch it back to check audio levels and clarity before recording your full scene.
</Tip>

### Dialogue delivery

**Pacing**

- Don't rush through dialogue
- Leave brief pauses between character exchanges
- Natural pacing helps the lip sync look better

**Consistency**

- Keep each character's voice consistent throughout your movie
- Make distinct voice choices for different toys
- Energy and enthusiasm come through in the final result

**Avoid audio pollution**

- Don't tap, rustle, or make noise while talking
- Minimize breathing sounds and mouth clicks
- Keep handling sounds quiet when moving toys

## Handling edge cases

Sometimes things don't go as planned. Here's how to handle common challenges.

### When segmentation isn't clean

**Problem: Hands appearing in the final cut**

- Solution: Hold toys from underneath or behind
- Use quicker movements where hands are only briefly visible
- Re-record the segment with better hand placement

**Problem: Parts of the toy are cut off**

- Solution: Ensure higher contrast between toy and background
- Improve lighting to eliminate shadows
- Use a solid-colored backdrop that contrasts with the toy

**Problem: Toy tracking is lost mid-scene**

- Solution: Keep the toy visible in frame at all times
- Avoid moving too quickly or rotating the toy drastically
- Make sure the toy doesn't match the background color

<Note>
  If a toy consistently has segmentation issues, try a different toy or adjust your setup (lighting, background, camera angle).
</Note>

### When backgrounds don't match expectations

**Generated background doesn't fit the story**

- Provide more specific descriptions to the voice agent
- Include style keywords ("cartoon," "realistic," "painterly")
- Reference atmosphere and mood explicitly

**Background is too busy or distracting**

- Ask for simpler, more minimal backgrounds
- Use descriptors like "clean," "simple," "uncluttered"
- Focus on one or two key environmental elements

**Background style varies between scenes**

- Keep notes on your background descriptions for consistency
- Use the same style keywords across all scenes in a movie
- Review previous scenes before describing new ones

### When multiple toys are difficult to manage

**Toys overlap and segmentation struggles**

- Stage the action so toys are separated
- Use camera framing to your advantage (one toy on left, one on right)
- Have toys interact without directly overlapping

**Can't tell which toy is speaking**

- Move the speaking toy slightly during dialogue
- Keep non-speaking toys still
- Use different voice pitches/styles for each character

## Advanced creative techniques

### Camera framing strategies

Even with a fixed camera, you can create visual variety:

**Close-ups**

- Move the toy closer to the camera for important moments
- Creates emphasis and emotional impact
- Good for dramatic dialogue or reactions

**Wide shots**

- Show the full environment and multiple toys
- Establishes setting and spatial relationships
- Good for action scenes and establishing shots

**Rule of thirds**

- Position toys off-center for more dynamic composition
- Place important action at the intersections of imaginary grid lines
- Feels more cinematic than always centering subjects

<Tip>
  Create different "shots" by recording separate scenes at different distances, then combine them in your final movie.
</Tip>

### Scene transitions

**Cut between scenes effectively**

- End one scene with action or dialogue that leads to the next
- Start new scenes with different camera distance or toy position
- Use location changes to show story progression

**Creating montages**

- Record several short clips of related action
- Combine them to show passage of time or training sequences
- Keep each clip brief (3-5 seconds) for quick pacing

### Special effects with backgrounds

**Weather and atmosphere**

- Request specific weather in background descriptions (rainy, snowy, sunny)
- Add dramatic lighting cues ("sunset," "moonlight," "stormy")
- Create mood through environmental details

**Time of day**

- Vary backgrounds to show morning, afternoon, evening, or night
- Shows story progression across time
- Different lighting creates different emotional tones

**Fantasy and impossible settings**

- Don't limit yourself to realistic locations
- Try clouds, inside a volcano, floating islands, alien planets
- The toy movie format invites creative, imaginative worlds

## Optimizing for processing time

Cinemachine processes your video to create the final result. Here's how to keep processing efficient:

### Scene length guidelines

- Aim for 30-90 seconds per scene for reasonable processing time
- Multiple short scenes process faster than one very long scene
- You can combine scenes afterward for the final movie

### Resolution and file size

- 1080p is the sweet spot for quality vs. processing time
- Higher resolutions (4K) take significantly longer to process
- Compress video files if they're very large (over 500MB)

### Planning for efficiency

- Record all scenes with the same background consecutively
- Minimize the number of different backgrounds per movie (backgrounds are generated per scene)
- Group similar scenes together in your workflow

<Note>
  Processing time varies based on scene complexity, number of toys, and video length. Typical scenes process in a few minutes.
</Note>

## Iteration and improvement

Your first movie might not be perfect — that's expected! Here's how to improve over time.

### Review and learn

- Watch your completed movies critically
- Note what worked well and what didn't
- Experiment with different approaches

### Build a personal playbook

- Keep notes on which toys work best
- Document successful background descriptions
- Save effective character voice choices

### Collaborate and share

- Show your movies to friends and family for feedback
- Ask what they found engaging or confusing
- Learn from other Cinemachine creators

<Tip>
  Every movie you make teaches you something. Embrace experimentation and have fun with the creative process!
</Tip>

## Troubleshooting checklist

Before recording, verify:

- [ ] Camera is stable and won't move
- [ ] Lighting is even across the play area
- [ ] Background contrasts with toy colors
- [ ] Reference frame captured with empty scene
- [ ] Audio is clear in test recording
- [ ] Toys are selected and tested for visibility
- [ ] Story scenes are planned out

During recording:

- [ ] Speaking clearly and at moderate pace
- [ ] Minimizing hand-toy contact time
- [ ] Moving toys smoothly without blur
- [ ] Keeping toys fully visible in frame
- [ ] Maintaining consistent energy and voice
